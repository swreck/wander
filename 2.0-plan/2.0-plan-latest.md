# Wander 2.0: The Experience

## What This Document Is

This is the build plan for Wander 2.0, written from the perspective of the people who will use it. Each section describes what someone sees, feels, and does — then, at the end of each section, the technical work required to make it real.

Once approved, all work happens on a `2.0` git branch. Main stays untouched.

---

## The Three People Using Wander

**Admin** — Ken (and anyone Ken designates). Full control. Sees everything: group interest signals, planning gaps, learnings from past trips. The person who turns a collection of ideas into a real itinerary.

**Planner** — Larisa, or anyone an Admin or Planner promotes. Can do everything an Admin can within the trip: add and schedule activities, build days, manage members. The difference is access-level, not capability — Planners are trusted collaborators.

**Explorer** — Everyone else. They browse what's planned, react to ideas, add their own finds, talk to Scout. They experience the trip through Wander without needing to understand how planning works. Their participation is welcome but never required. Silence is fine.

---

## What 2.0 Is NOT

- Not a rewrite. The backend and data model stay. This is a UX transformation.
- Not social. No public profiles, no sharing beyond the trip group.
- Not gamified. No badges, streaks, or engagement metrics.
- Not Scout-as-boss. Every suggestion dismissable. Every flow completable without Scout.

## Build 1: A Day That Reads Like a Day

### What it feels like now
You open a day and see a database: promoted experiences, then possible experiences, then reservations, then accommodations. Four sections that reflect how data is stored, not how a day unfolds.

### What it becomes
You open a day and see your day. A continuous vertical flow from morning to evening, with subtle background shading (warm gold for morning, soft white for midday, gentle blue-grey for evening) that gives a sense of time passing — not rigid buckets, but a feeling.

**What you see, top to bottom:**
- Your accommodation (if you're checking in today) as a soft header card
- Activities and reservations placed along the timeline at their approximate times
- Reservations (with confirmed times) feel solid — they anchor the day
- Planned activities (without exact times) float in their general time window, positioned but not locked
- Between items, gentle "open" indicators show free time — not empty space, but breathing room. For Admins and Planners, tapping an open slot shows nearby saved ideas from the city, or opens Scout with context
- Your accommodation checkout at the bottom (if departing)

**Personal items:** Any person can add private reminders to their own timeline — "check gift shop for something for John" — that only they see. These appear in a lighter style alongside the shared plan. Your day is the group's day plus your own notes.

**For Explorers:** Just read top to bottom. Morning through evening. That's it. No planning vocabulary to learn.

**The key shift:** Possible experiences — things saved to the city but not yet scheduled — disappear from the day view entirely. A day shows what's happening, not what might happen. The city is where you browse and dream (Build 2). The day is where you execute.

### Technical scope
- New `DayTimeline.tsx` component replacing current day content in `PlanPage.tsx`
- Continuous layout with CSS background gradients for time-of-day shading
- `PersonalItem` model: travelerId, dayId, content, timeWindow, private: true
- Existing data model unchanged — experiences keep `timeWindow` and `dayId`
- Reservation times drive vertical positioning
- Accommodation check-in/check-out as bookend cards
- Untouched: map, bottom nav, drawer system, Scout chat

## Build 2: The City as a Place, Not a List

### What it feels like now
A city's experiences appear as filtered lists within the day view. You see them organized by planning status (promoted vs. possible), not by what kind of experience they are or how excited people are about them.

### What it becomes
Each city gets its own page — an idea board that feels like the city itself.

**The header:** A full-width photo of the city (from Cloudinary). The city name, date range (or "5 days" for dateless trips), and your accommodation name and address. This isn't a data display — it's an arrival. You should feel like you're looking at a place, not a spreadsheet.

**The ideas, grouped by theme:** Food spots together. Temples together. Art together. Each idea is a card showing:
- The name
- Who saved it (contributor color dot)
- Rating if available
- A subtle check if it's already scheduled on a day

The grouping makes it easy to scan by interest. "What are our food options?" is answered by looking at one section, not scrolling a flat list.

**Reactions — the iMessage model:**
On any idea card, you can tap to react. Three quick-access emoji: heart, thumbs up, fire. Plus an option to add any emoji you want. Tap once to add, tap again to remove.

This is comfortably optional. No empty-state shaming. The Admin and Planners see aggregate counts ("3 reactions") but never see who DIDN'T react. Non-participation is invisible. Absence of input is privacy, not disengagement.

**Quick notes on ideas:**
Below any card, you can add a short note: "My friend said this place is incredible" or "Closed on Mondays." Attributed to you, visible to the group. Not a review — more like a text to the group chat about a place.

**Bottom of the board:** "Add an idea" (opens capture) and "Ask Scout about this city" (opens chat with the city pre-loaded as context).

**For the Admin/Planner:** The board is where you read the room. Which ideas have energy? Which have notes? This is signal for day-building — not a formal vote, just a natural sense of what the group cares about. If you schedule something with zero reactions and skip something with five, nothing warns you. Your call.

**For the Explorer:** Browse, react to things that look fun, add your own finds. You don't need to understand days, timelines, or scheduling. This is your main surface.

### Technical scope
- New `CityBoard.tsx` page component
- New `IdeaCard.tsx` component with reaction system
- New `ExperienceReaction` model in Prisma (travelerId, experienceId, emoji: string)
- New `ExperienceNote` model in Prisma (travelerId, experienceId, content: string)
- API endpoints: reactions (POST/DELETE), notes (POST/PATCH/DELETE)
- Scout tools: `get_city_interests` (reaction summaries), `get_trip_activity` (recent actions)
- Navigation: tapping a city name opens the city board
- Existing Decision/Vote system stays for formal group choices — different purpose

## Build 3: A Dashboard That Knows What Time It Is

### What it feels like now
The TripOverview is the same screen whether your trip is six months away or happening right now. Calendar grid, experience lists, same layout regardless of context.

### What it becomes
The dashboard adapts to where you are in the trip lifecycle. Not different apps — the same warm home screen, but showing what matters most right now.

**When you're dreaming (dates TBD):**
- Trip name, member faces
- Cities as reorderable cards — "First stop, second stop, third stop"
- Each city card shows: how many ideas saved, how many reactions, whether accommodation is set
- Big, inviting actions: "Add ideas" and "Invite people"
- Scout might offer one thought (always dismissable): "You've got three cities but no restaurants saved yet"

**When you're planning (dates set, trip is 2+ weeks away):**
- Calendar grid where each day shows its character — a few theme emojis that represent what's scheduled (a temple icon and a noodle bowl, not a text label like "Temple day" — because most days are mixed and a single label oversimplifies)
- Days without activities have a soft visual warmth — not alarming, just noticeable
- A gentle progress sense: "18 of 24 days have something planned"
- Coverage at a glance: "Hanoi has lots of ideas but Ho Chi Minh City needs attention"

**When it's getting real (under 2 weeks out):**
- Everything from planning, plus:
- Weather outlook for the trip dates
- Document readiness: "2 people haven't shared passport info yet"
- Practical nudges based on what's scheduled: "You have 2 outdoor days — worth checking the forecast"

**When you're there (during the trip):**
- Today's timeline front and center — the Build 1 day view, embedded
- Tomorrow as a compact preview below
- Active alerts: weather shifts, transit disruptions, "leave in 20 minutes for your 2pm reservation"

**When you're home (after the trip):**
- A warm summary: cities visited, days lived, things you did, who contributed what
- "Want to capture your trip story?" (links to Build 7)
- "Ready for the next one?"

### Technical scope
- Phase detection: compare trip dates to `new Date()`, check `datesKnown`
- Refactor `TripOverview.tsx` into phase-specific sub-components
- Calendar day cells: compute dominant theme emojis from scheduled activities
- New `PlanningProgress.tsx` component
- Reusable `ScoutNudge.tsx` component
- Stays: map, bottom nav, trip switching, approval queue, learnings panels

## Build 4: Now — Your Travel-Aware Friend

### What it feels like now
The Now screen shows today's timeline during the trip. Before and after the trip, it's mostly empty.

### What it becomes
Now becomes the screen that always has something useful to say — before, during, and after.

**Before the trip (planning phase):**
Not a task list. Not a project manager. More like a friend who's been looking at your plans and has a few thoughts.

The insights lean toward things a human planner might miss — spatial and logistical awareness, not obvious gaps:
- "Your hotel is in the Old Quarter. 4 of your saved spots are walking distance, but the museum and the cooking class are across the river — that's a 30-minute cab each way."
- "December 27 has 6 activities. The temple and the market alone are a half day — you might be running."
- "The cooking class has reactions from 5 people. Might be worth locking in."

Each insight is a card you can tap (goes to the relevant day or city) or dismiss (never comes back). The tone is "hey, in case it's useful" — never "you need to fix this."

**A preview of what Now becomes during travel:**
At the bottom of pre-trip Now, a small section shows what this screen will look like during the trip — using your first planned day's real data. "During your trip, this is where you'll see your live timeline, walking distances, and when to leave for your next thing."

**During the trip:**
- Today's full timeline — the same Build 1 view, but here it's the entire focus. No calendar, no city overview, just today. (Other views are one tap away via bottom nav.)
- "What's next" with distance and estimated travel time
- Your position on the map relative to your next destination
- Morning brief: appears once each morning, dismissable. Today's weather, today's plan at a glance, any alerts. This is also where the personal "mint on the pillow" appears — a detail tied to your interests, different for each person. If you're into ceramics, Scout might mention that the neighborhood you're in today has a pottery tradition. If you're a food person, a note about the best time to hit the market.
- When conditions change: "Your afternoon hike might get rained out. The ceramics studio is nearby and indoors."

**After the trip:**
- Trip stats: days, cities, activities completed, distance walked (from phone GPS/health data — most people with Apple Watches will have this)
- Learnings captured during the trip — things you told Scout to remember, or reflections you left
- "Create your trip story" link

### Map UX audit (included in this build)
The map has accumulated features without a holistic pass: theme emoji filtering, walkable circle, three marker tiers, GPS dot, overlapping pins. This build includes a UX review of the map to:
- Resolve visual clutter when many pins overlap
- Clarify the distinction between planned markers and suggestions
- Ensure the walkable circle and theme filtering work together cleanly
- Make the GPS dot and "return to location" button feel natural, not bolted on

### Technical scope
- Refactor `NowPage.tsx` to be phase-aware
- New `PlanningInsight.tsx` component for pre-trip cards
- Backend endpoint: `GET /api/trips/:id/insights` analyzes trip structure, returns observations
- Morning brief: time-based card during trip dates, includes personalized Easter egg from traveler preferences
- Map audit: review and refactor marker rendering, circle overlay, filter interactions
- During-trip Now is largely existing code, reorganized

## Build 5: Scout as a Presence, Not a Panel

### What it feels like now
Scout lives in a chat panel you open. You go to Scout when you want something. Otherwise, Scout is invisible.

### What it becomes
Scout is still in the chat panel for open-ended conversation. But Scout also appears contextually — small, dismissable thoughts on the surfaces where they're relevant.

**On the day timeline, at a free-time slot:**
"There are 3 saved restaurants near your morning temple. Want one for lunch?"

**On the city board, when a theme is crowded:**
"You have 6 food spots saved for Hanoi. Want me to help pick the best 3?"

**On the dashboard, when a pattern emerges:**
"Days 3-5 are packed but Day 6 is empty. Want to spread things out?"

**On Now, when conditions shift:**
"Your afternoon hike might get rained out. The ceramics studio is nearby and indoors."

**From past trips:**
When you're looking at a city that has relevant learnings from a previous trip, a small card: "From Japan 2026: big groups struggled at formal restaurants." Not blocking — just a note.

**Inline actions:**
- Long-press any experience card: "Ask Scout about this" opens chat with context pre-filled
- On the city board: "What should we do in Hanoi?" opens chat with city context

**The absolute constraint:** Every nudge dismisses with one gesture. No dismissed nudge ever reappears. No "Scout thinks you should..." framing — always "in case it's useful." Scout never blocks an action or requires interaction before you can proceed. Scout is the local friend who has a thought, not the tour guide who won't let you cross the street.

### Technical scope
- New `ScoutNudge.tsx` component (reusable across all surfaces)
- Backend: `POST /api/scout/suggestions` takes context (current view, trip state, time), returns 0-2 suggestions via Claude
- Nudge dismissal in localStorage (lightweight, private, not server state)
- Existing chat panel unchanged — these are additions

## Build 6: The Group Comes Alive

### What it feels like now
You know your own activity. Other people's contributions show via colored dots on experiences. But there's no sense of the group as a living thing — no pulse of what's happening.

### What it becomes

**Activity feed:**
A lightweight stream of what's been happening. Not push notifications — a feed you check when you're curious.

- "Larisa added Cafe Pho Co to Hanoi"
- "Brian reacted to the cooking class"
- "Ken scheduled Day 3: Old Quarter walking tour, Bun Cha for lunch"
- "Ava joined the trip"

Visible as a small "recent activity" section on the dashboard, expandable to a full page. The feed only shows positive actions — things people did. Never what people didn't do. No "Brian hasn't added any ideas yet." Absence is invisible.

**Reactions and notes (from Build 2) come alive here:**
The city board's reaction and note system generates feed items. When Brian reacts to the cooking class, it shows up in the feed. When Larisa adds a note ("my friend said this place is incredible"), it shows up too. The group feels present even when you're planning alone.

**For Admins and Planners:**
The city board shows a subtle overlay of group energy. Which ideas have the most reactions. Which have notes. Which have nothing. This is information, not instruction. If you schedule something with zero reactions, nothing warns you. Freedom, not constraint.

### Technical scope
- `ActivityFeed` model auto-generated from ChangeLog + reaction events
- `ActivityFeed.tsx` component
- API endpoints for feed retrieval
- Scout tool: `get_trip_activity`

## Build 7: After the Trip — Memory and Story

### What it feels like now
The trip ends. Wander has nothing to say. The data sits there, but there's no way to look back meaningfully.

### What it becomes
Wander becomes valuable after the trip, not just during planning.

**End-of-day reflections (during the trip):**
In the evening (after 6pm local time, during trip dates), a soft card appears: "How was today?"

You can:
- Tap a reaction on any activity from today to mark it as a highlight
- Add one or more photos or videos (standard file picker, works in Safari)
- Say something to Scout — voice or text — and it's saved as a memory: "the cooking class was incredible, the instructor told us about her grandmother's recipe"
- Or swipe the card away. No guilt. No "you haven't reflected on 3 days." Dismissed means dismissed — for that day, forever.

**The trip story (after the trip ends):**
A card on the dashboard: "Welcome home. Want to look back?"

The trip story is something new — not a slideshow, not a PDF, not a social media post. It's a scrollable, interactive narrative. A living document that you build and customize.

**What the story contains:**
- City by city, day by day
- Photos and videos matched to activities — manually added via daily reflections, or bulk-added post-trip ("Pick photos from Dec 25 to Jan 1 and I'll match them to your days by timestamp")
- Learnings woven in: "Day 3 taught you: book restaurants 2 weeks ahead for groups over 6"
- Contributor credit: "Larisa found Cafe Pho Co, which became the group's morning spot"
- Highlights from daily reflections featured prominently
- Stats: "8 days, 2 cities, 47 activities, 12 restaurants, 3 temples"
- A map trace showing your route through each city

**Two versions:**
- **Personal:** Your photos, your highlights, activities you attended. The one you open on a quiet evening.
- **Group:** Even coverage of people and places. Shared highlights. The one you send to the family group chat.

**Customization:**
Every card in the story is editable — reorder by dragging, remove with a swipe, add captions, expand or collapse sections. The story is yours to shape.

**Sharing and saving:**
The group story lives at a permanent, beautiful web page — a single link you can send to anyone. It works in any browser, needs no app.

For saving: "Save as PDF" gives you a printable file. But the web page is the real artifact — bookmark it, add it to your home screen, share the link in the family group chat. It's not ephemeral — it lives as long as Wander does.

**Photo and video handling:**
- During trip: daily reflection offers multi-select file picker for photos and videos
- Post-trip: "Your trip ran Dec 25 to Jan 1. Pick photos from that period." Multi-select, Wander matches to days by timestamp, you confirm placement
- Future (Capacitor native): automatic camera roll scanning by date range

### Technical scope
- New `Reflection` model (dayId, travelerId, highlights: string[], mediaUrls: string[], note?)
- New `TripStory` model (tripId, type: "personal"|"group", sections: JSON, published: boolean, shareToken: string)
- `ReflectionCard.tsx` — evening prompt
- `TripStory.tsx` — interactive narrative viewer/editor
- `TripStoryGenerator` service — assembles narrative from trip data, reflections, media
- Media upload to Cloudinary (existing infrastructure, extended for video)
- Shareable story page: public URL with read-only view
- PDF export via html2pdf or similar
- Scout tools: `create_trip_story`, `add_reflection`

## Build 8: The First 60 Seconds

### What it feels like now
New users land on a login page, enter a code, see a trip overview. No context, no warmth, no sense of what this thing is.

### What it becomes
When someone taps their invite link for the first time, they land in context — not a feature tour.

**Step 1: "Here's what's happening"**
A visual summary — not raw data, but a narrative card:
"The Fong Family Vietnam trip: 8 days across Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City. 15 activities saved so far, from street food tours to temple visits. Larisa added a cooking class yesterday that 4 people are excited about."

This gives a new person an immediate sense of scale, energy, and what the group has been up to. Not a review of all the planning work — just enough to feel oriented.

**Step 2: "A few things about you"**
If the Admin has already mentioned this person's interests to Scout, the screen prepopulates: "Ken mentioned you're into ceramics and cooking. Sound right?" with the ability to adjust.

If not, a short Scout conversation — 2-3 questions max:
- "What kind of experiences do you love?" (tappable interest tags, framed conversationally)
- "Anything you definitely want to do in Vietnam?" (free text — Scout saves it as an idea)

Each question skippable. "Skip — you can always tell Scout later."

**Step 3: Land on the dashboard with a Scout message**
"Based on what you told me, you might especially like the cooking class and the Cu Chi Tunnels. Take a look?" Links directly to those items on the city board.

**Return visitors:** No onboarding replay. Dashboard loads with their last-viewed trip. If they haven't set preferences, Scout might surface one gentle question in a nudge sometime — not immediately, not urgently.

### Technical scope
- Refactor `NewMemberOnboarding.tsx` for three-step flow
- `GET /api/trips/:id/summary` — narrative summary generation
- Scout conversational onboarding (scripted questions + free-text)
- Preference prepopulation from existing traveler data
- Preference-to-recommendation matching in step 3

## Build 9: One Button to Capture Anything

### What it feels like now
Five entry points for capture: paste, drop, camera, chat, import. Powerful but scattered.

### What it becomes
A single floating action button (FAB) on every screen.

- **Tap:** Opens camera
- **Long-press:** Three options — Paste text, Enter URL, Voice ("Say what you found")
- **Voice:** "Save Bun Cha Huong Lien, it's Obama's pho place in Hanoi." Scout extracts the name, associates it with Hanoi, enriches it. Confirmation only if something's ambiguous.

The FAB knows where you are — if you're on the Hanoi city board, it pre-fills Hanoi as context. The entire existing capture pipeline (AI extraction, geocoding, enrichment) still runs underneath. This is just the entry point, simplified.

### Technical scope
- New `CaptureFAB.tsx` component
- Voice capture via existing Web Speech API
- Context-aware city pre-fill
- Remove redundant capture buttons from other surfaces

## Build 10: Voice and Tone Audit

### What this is
Everything is built. Now read every user-facing string — new and old — through Ken's voice principles and the Wander tone guide.

**The test:** Would you say this out loud at a small table to a smart friend? Does it make them lean in or look for the exit?

- Every Scout nudge, every empty state, every confirmation, every toast
- The 16 negative rules from Ken's voice system (no rhetorical questions, no metaphorical verbs, no marketing buzzwords, no urgency phrasing)
- Scout's voice verified as "local friend" — never "AI assistant"
- Database/programmer language removed from any surface a user sees
- Fix in the same pass, don't flag for later

### Technical scope
- Read every component, extract user-visible strings
- Apply voice rules, fix violations
- Verify Scout system prompt reflects the local-friend voice
